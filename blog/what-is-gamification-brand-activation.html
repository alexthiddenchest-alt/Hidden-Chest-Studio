What Is Gamification for Brand Activation?

A straight answer to what gamification actually means, how it differs from a branded minigame, and what it takes to build an engagement system that works — from the Kuala Lumpur studio behind AirAsia's LOFINDER and Khazanah Nasional's Carcosa Seri Negara installation.

Hidden Chest Studio Published July 8, 2026 Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia

What Is Gamification?

Gamification is the practice of applying game design mechanics — points, progress, rewards, competition, feedback loops — to a product, app, or campaign that isn't itself a game, in order to increase engagement and retention.

The goal isn't to turn a banking app or a loyalty programme into a video game. It's to borrow the specific mechanics that make games compelling — a visible sense of progress, a reason to come back tomorrow, a reward that feels earned rather than random — and apply them to a real business objective. Done well, gamification changes user behaviour without the audience necessarily thinking of it as "a game" at all. How Is Gamification Different From a Branded Minigame?

A branded minigame is a standalone game built around a campaign; gamification is the broader practice of layering game mechanics — points, rewards, progress — onto an existing product or experience, and a branded minigame is often just one component inside a larger gamification strategy.

The two overlap constantly but aren't the same thing. If a brand needs a discrete, playable experience for a campaign — something people load up, play for a few minutes, and share — that's a branded minigame. If a brand needs an ongoing system that changes how users behave inside an app, a loyalty programme, or an internal tool over weeks or months, that's gamification. Many projects need both: a minigame can be the centrepiece of a wider gamified activation that also includes a leaderboard, rewards tier, or referral mechanic.

How Does Gamification Work for Brand Activation?

Gamification drives brand activation by giving an audience a reason to opt in and keep participating, rather than passively receiving a message — the mechanics create a loop of action, feedback, and reward that a static campaign can't replicate.

This is the process behind LOFINDER, the gamification project we built for AirAsia: instead of a conventional promotion, the campaign gave the audience an activity built around AirAsia's brand, with the mechanics designed first and the interface built around them — not the other way around. That sequencing matters. A lot of "gamified" products fail because a points counter gets bolted onto an existing flow after the fact. We design the engagement loop first, using real game design principles, then build the rewards system and interface around it.

Gamification for brand activation isn't limited to apps and screens, either. For Khazanah Nasional, we applied the same engagement-first thinking to a bespoke, physical interactive installation at the historic Carcosa Seri Negara — proof that the discipline transfers from a mobile screen to a live, in-venue experience.

What Kinds of Gamification Systems Can Be Built?

Gamification systems range from gamified mobile and web apps, to enterprise engagement and rewards platforms, to physical on-site installations — the right format depends on where the audience already spends time and what behaviour the brand is trying to change.

  • Gamified apps: consumer-facing mobile or web apps with progress tracking, rewards, and competitive mechanics built into the core flow.
  • Enterprise engagement systems: internal or customer-facing platforms using gamification to drive adoption, training completion, or repeat use.
  • Rewards and loyalty mechanics: point systems, tiered rewards, and progress mechanics layered onto an existing product or programme.
  • Physical installations: on-site, kiosk- or screen-based gamified experiences for events and venues, like the Carcosa Seri Negara project.

How Much Does a Gamification Project Cost?

Cost depends on scope — a lightweight gamified layer added to an existing app costs far less than a full enterprise engagement platform or a bespoke physical installation, so pricing should be scoped against the specific system being built rather than a single flat rate.

The main cost drivers are the number of mechanics involved (points, tiers, leaderboards, referral systems), whether it's a new build or layered onto an existing product, and whether backend infrastructure like user accounts or analytics needs to be built alongside it. Our interactive installation for Khazanah Nasional, for instance, involved bespoke hardware and software — a very different scope from a gamified layer added to an existing mobile app. The fastest way to get a real number is to tell us your brief and we'll scope it directly.
